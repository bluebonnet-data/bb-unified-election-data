The Problem

Ever-changing maps

When states redraw their political maps, directly comparing election results across cycles becomes challenging. You are looking at different slices of the population each time. A district might appear more Republican or Democratic simply because different neighborhoods got added or removed, not because of any real shift in how people voted.

This project aims to address exactly that.

The Approach

Population-weighted
interpolation

Election results are reported at the precinct level. Precincts are the small geographic units, roughly neighborhood-sized, where votes are counted and recorded. They are always the starting point.

When district lines are redrawn, the new boundaries rarely align with old precinct lines. A single precinct might now straddle two different congressional districts. To reconstruct what past elections would have looked like under the new map, you need to split that precinct's votes accurately.

This pipeline uses three ingredients:

Precinct boundaries, the old map, where election results live.

New district boundaries, the target geography you want to analyze.

Census block population data, the bridge between the two. Census blocks are tiny geographic units. The Census assigns an exact population count to each one. They tell you where people actually live inside each precinct.

By overlaying census blocks onto both maps, the pipeline calculates what fraction of each precinct's population falls inside each new district. Those fractions become the weights that allocate historical votes to each new district.

Proof of Concept

Travis County, TX — Phase 1 Pilot

Texas redrew its congressional maps in 2025. Travis County (Austin) served as our proof of concept, illustrating exactly the kind of structural shift this pipeline is designed to quantify.

District 35 was eliminated entirely in the 2025 redraw. Its replacement, District 11, gives Travis County voters only 19% of the district's population. Meanwhile, District 37 became 98% Travis County, concentrated but not influential across the surrounding competitive seats. The pipeline quantified this shift before a single 2026 ballot was cast, with zero vote leakage across all 247 Travis County precincts.

What We Are Building

Phase 2 and beyond

Phase 1 proved the methodology works in Travis County. The next steps scale it into a tool campaigns can actually use.

Time series: Adding 2016, 2018, 2022, and 2024 election cycles so campaigns can see trends, not just snapshots.

County scaling: Generalizing the pipeline to a second Texas county to validate it works across different geographies.

Statewide architecture: Designing the database framework to handle all 254 Texas counties.

Dashboard: The weights tables are the foundation. Campaigns, organizers, researchers, and engaged citizens will be able to pull up any congressional district and see its full electoral history visualized on today's map, making it clear what is a real political shift and what is simply the result of lines being redrawn.
