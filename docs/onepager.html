The Unified Election
Data Project

A spatial data pipeline that translates historical election results onto newly redrawn maps — separating true neighborhood political trends from structural shifts caused by changing district lines.

Python / GeoPandas GIS / Spatial Analysis Census Data Open Source Texas 2026

The Problem

When states redraw their political maps, directly comparing election results across cycles becomes challenging. You are looking at different slices of the population each time. A district might appear more Republican or Democratic simply because different neighborhoods got added or removed — not because of any real shift in how people voted.

This project aims to address exactly that.

"The lines moved. Did voters actually change — or does it just look that way?"
This project answers that question with precision, for any district, before a single new ballot is cast.
The Approach

Population-weighted
interpolation

Election results are reported at the precinct level. Precincts are the small geographic units — roughly neighborhood-sized — where votes are counted and recorded. They are always the starting point.

When district lines are redrawn, the new boundaries rarely align with old precinct lines. A single precinct might now straddle two different congressional districts. To reconstruct what past elections would have looked like under the new map, you need to split that precinct's votes accurately.

This pipeline uses three ingredients:

Precinct boundaries — the old map, where election results live.

New district boundaries — the target geography you want to analyze.

Census block population data — the bridge between the two. Census blocks are tiny geographic units each with an exact population count. They tell you where people actually live inside each precinct.

By overlaying census blocks onto both maps, the pipeline calculates what fraction of each precinct's population falls inside each new district. Those fractions become the weights that allocate historical votes to each new district.

Proof of Concept

Travis County, TX — Phase 1 Pilot

Texas redrew its congressional maps in 2025. Travis County (Austin) became a test case for exactly the kind of structural shift this pipeline is designed to quantify.

What the redistricting actually did to Travis County

247 Travis County precincts in the pilot
38% → 19% Travis County's share of District 35, now replaced by District 11
0.0 Vote leakage — exact preservation of all vote totals

Under the 2021 map, Travis County held 38% of District 35's population — a meaningful competitive seat where Austin-area voters had real influence. The 2026 map eliminated District 35 entirely. Its replacement, District 11, gives Travis County only 19% of the population. Meanwhile, District 37 became 98% Travis County — concentrated, not influential across competitive seats.

What We Are Building Next

Phase 2 and beyond

Phase 1 proved the methodology works in Travis County. The next steps scale it into a production-grade tool.

Time series (Issue #10): Adding 2016, 2018, 2022, and 2024 election cycles so campaigns can see trends, not just snapshots.

County scaling (Issue #11): Generalizing the pipeline to a second Texas county to validate it works across different geographies.

Statewide architecture (Issue #12): Designing the database framework to handle all 254 Texas counties.

Dashboard: The weights tables are the foundation. Once built, campaigns, organizers, researchers, and engaged citizens can pull up any congressional district and see its full electoral history visualized on today's map — making it clear what is a real political shift and what is simply the result of lines being redrawn.
